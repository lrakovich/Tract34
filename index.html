Tract 34 of Providence, RI, known as the Blackstone Park Historic District, can be broken roughly into three vertical strips. Going from West to East, you would first pass through a heavily residential area where life, or at least living, is the main purpose – beautiful homes, people walking along the boulevard, cyclists riding by, and hardly any places of work or commerce. Continuing westward, you would reach Swan Point Cemetery, a historical cemetery established in 1846. Despite its spacious appearance on the map, in stark contrast to the tightly-packed residential streets, it’s home to over forty-thousand residents. Further beyond is the Seekonk River.

As we explored the three parts of Blackstone, we gradually began noticing that their differences highlight their commonalities, and vice versa. Signs of life abound in the residential streets and Swan Point’s avenues, both as literal testaments of lives being lived and having had lived, and as systems of symbols, asynchronous conversations being carried through space and time. In a way, the cemetery is a place where distance is flattened, each gravestone a portal where multiple points become one. The effects of time can be seen and heard everywhere, as wind rustles through the leaves in quiet front yards, as wind erodes stone, as stones cast in water send ripples along its surface, spreading wider, wider, until they become flush with the surface once again, no longer seen, and the stones remain hidden, there and not-there.

This is a census of the living and the dead.

—Louis Rakovich & Zoey Guo, 2020

Mythology

——Arcturus

The name Arcturus is derived from the Greek word Αρκτοῦρος, which means "Guardian of the Bear".

In mythology, Zeus fell in love with Lycaon’s daughter, Callisto, and gave birth to Arcas. Zeus's wife Hera knew this and turned Callisto into a bear. When Arcas grew up, he reunited in Kassto in the forest, but he didn't know that the bear was his mother. He aimed his arrow at the bear. At this time, Zeus transformed Arcas into a bear like his mother and put them into the sky. They became Ursa Major and Ursa Minor. Arcturus was created to guard them.

Ursa Major is often used as an important navigational sign. Observers in the northern hemisphere can find Arcturus along the direction indicated by the arc of Ursa Major. If you find Ursa Major in the night sky, you can always find your way home.
